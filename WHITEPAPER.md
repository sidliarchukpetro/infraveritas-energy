## 1. Executive Summary

The biggest unsolved problem in real-world asset tokenization isn't financial. It's physical.

Blockchains are extraordinary at preserving truth once it has been written. They are powerless to verify whether the truth being written corresponds to anything real. For tokenized treasury bills or stablecoins, this asymmetry is manageable — financial instruments live and die in digital ledgers, and the on-chain record is the canonical reality. For tokenized real-world assets — energy generation, infrastructure performance, environmental compliance, physical commodities — the asymmetry is fatal. Every existing protocol resolves it the same way: by trusting a human attestor to bridge the gap. That bridge is fragile, expensive, and structurally incompatible with the scale that real-world asset tokenization is supposed to unlock.

**InfraVeritas Energy is the first protocol to solve the physical oracle problem at the protocol layer.** Sensor measurements are signed at the device by hardware-rooted keys, committed via cryptographic hashing at the edge, and submitted on-chain together with a zero-knowledge proof binding the signed measurement to the public commitment. The on-chain verifier confirms — without trust in any human operator — that the measurement originated from an authorized device, has not been altered in transit, and is consistent with the device's stated identity and authorization scope. The result is a piece of physical-world data that any smart contract on Ethereum can consume as if it had originated on-chain in the first place.

The protocol is **live on Sepolia today**. Edge devices, aggregator orchestration, ZK proof generation, and on-chain verification all run end-to-end. The supporting IPAS standard (Infrastructure Physical Asset Standard, v1.0) is under USPTO patent protection. The codebase has cleared a comprehensive pre-audit security review with no critical findings.

## 2. The Physical Oracle Problem

> *Today's real-world asset protocols ask blockchains to verify what humans say happened. We are asking them to verify what actually happened.*

### 2.1 The asymmetry no one talks about

The tokenization thesis is now mainstream. The Boston Consulting Group projects $10–16 trillion of real-world assets to be tokenized by 2030. BlackRock has launched on-chain treasury products. Franklin Templeton runs a tokenized money market fund. Ondo Finance has crossed a billion dollars in tokenized treasuries. The financial-instrument layer of tokenization works.

The physical-asset layer does not.

Tokenized solar generation, tokenized real estate income streams, tokenized carbon credits, tokenized infrastructure performance — every category that requires bridging the digital ledger to something happening in the physical world remains stuck in pilot phase, dependent on a handful of trusted intermediaries, and unable to scale. The reason is the same in every case: blockchains can preserve truth, but they cannot create it. They cannot measure voltage. They cannot witness construction quality. They cannot count kilowatt-hours generated by a solar array on a specific roof at a specific time.

To pretend otherwise, the industry has settled on a workaround that is rarely named directly: **trusted human attestors**. Notaries, certified auditors, accredited engineers, third-party verifiers. A licensed professional inspects the physical asset, signs a statement, and that signature becomes the bridge between the asset and its on-chain representation. Every existing protocol for real-world asset tokenization depends on this bridge.

The bridge is fragile.

### 2.2 Where the bridge breaks

A trusted attestor model has three structural weaknesses, each of which has been exploited or has failed in production within the last five years:

**Cost.** A licensed engineering inspection of a single mid-sized commercial solar installation costs between US $1,000 and US $5,000. The same range applies to most physical asset categories. At the scale tokenization promises — millions of assets, fractional ownership, continuous re-verification — the human cost dominates the underlying asset value. Tokenization stops being economically viable for any asset under approximately $250,000.

**Trust concentration.** A small number of attestors validate a disproportionately large share of tokenized assets. Their signatures are valuable; the incentive to compromise them is correspondingly large. The 2023 collapse of several voluntary carbon credit registries traced directly to attestor capture: third-party verifiers issued certifications that did not correspond to physical reality, and the on-chain tokens that depended on those certifications became worthless overnight.

**Verifiability gap.** Even when attestors act honestly, the on-chain consumer of a tokenized asset has no cryptographic way to verify that the attestor's signature corresponds to what the attestor actually observed. The trust is transitive — the smart contract trusts the registry, which trusts the attestor, who trusts the asset operator's documentation. Every link in that chain is a potential failure point, and none of them produces machine-verifiable evidence.

The result is that real-world asset tokenization has bifurcated. Financial instruments — where the digital ledger is the asset — scale freely. Physical assets — where the digital ledger is a claim about the asset — stall at the verification bottleneck.

### 2.3 The three layers of physical trust

A physical measurement is not a single event. It is a chain of three distinct trust transitions, each of which the attestor model collapses into a single signature:

**Device integrity.** Did the sensor that produced the measurement do so honestly? Was its firmware tampered with? Were its calibration parameters silently altered? Was the device physically opened and its outputs replaced? Today's attestation model assumes the device is honest because the attestor visually inspected it. There is no cryptographic record of device state.

**Measurement integrity.** Did the value that left the sensor reach the recording system unaltered? Network paths, gateway aggregators, and database write layers are each opportunities for manipulation. Today's model assumes integrity because the data went through "trusted infrastructure." There is no end-to-end binding between raw sensor output and stored measurement.

**Submission integrity.** Did the measurement that reached the recording system get reported to the verifier — registry, regulator, blockchain — without alteration? This is the layer attestors directly sign. It is also the only layer where they have direct visibility. The two preceding layers are inferred, not verified.

For a tokenized asset to be cryptographically meaningful, all three layers must produce machine-verifiable evidence. No production system today does this for physical measurements. The technology to do it has only recently become economically feasible.

### 2.4 Why the window is now

The reason no protocol has solved the physical oracle problem until now is not that the problem went unrecognized. It is that the components required to solve it became practical only in the last 24–36 months:

**Zero-knowledge proof systems** with proving times under one second and on-chain verification costs under 300,000 gas — the threshold at which per-measurement proofs become economic — only emerged with the Plonky2, Halo2, and Honk proof systems released between 2022 and 2025. Before that, ZK verification of arbitrary computation was research-grade infrastructure, not production tooling.

**Hardware security modules** with sufficient capability to perform on-device cryptographic signing, packaged in form factors deployable to commodity edge devices, became broadly available at consumer price points only after 2023. The Microchip ATECC608B, the NXP EdgeLock series, and similar parts placed device-rooted signing within the reach of any infrastructure project rather than requiring custom silicon.

**Permissioned tokenization standards** — particularly ERC-3643 and its compliance extensions — matured into deployable, audited primitives between 2024 and 2026. These standards provide the on-chain machinery for managing tokenized real-world assets within regulatory boundaries. Before they existed, even a solved oracle problem would have had nowhere to deliver its output.

The convergence of these three developments — economically viable ZK proofs, accessible hardware roots of trust, and production-grade compliance primitives — creates the first window in which a general-purpose physical oracle protocol can be both technically and economically deployable. InfraVeritas Energy is built squarely inside that window.

## 3. The InfraVeritas Approach

> *We collapse three layers of physical trust into a single cryptographic proof.*

### 3.1 Architectural principle

InfraVeritas Energy does not attempt to replace human attestors with better attestors. It eliminates the need for attestors at the verification layer. The protocol's central design principle is **end-to-end cryptographic binding**: every physical measurement carries, all the way to the smart contract that consumes it, machine-verifiable evidence that addresses each of the three trust layers identified in Section 2.3.

The principle is straightforward to state and difficult to engineer:

- The **device layer** is bound by hardware-rooted cryptographic identity.
- The **measurement layer** is bound by deterministic canonical commitment.
- The **submission layer** is bound by zero-knowledge proof of consistency.

A single signature, a single commitment, and a single proof, produced in that order at the edge of the network, travel together to the verifier. The verifier accepts only when all three layers are internally consistent and the device's identity is recognized by an on-chain registry. No human is in the verification path.

### 3.2 IPAS — Infrastructure Physical Asset Standard

The architectural principle is encoded in a formal standard: **IPAS v1.0** (Infrastructure Physical Asset Standard). IPAS specifies the data structures, cryptographic primitives, signing semantics, and verification rules that any compliant physical-asset oracle must follow. It is designed to be implementable independently — a second team could build a compatible IPAS device using the standard alone.

IPAS v1.0 is under USPTO patent protection (Provisional Application #63/876,031). The standard itself is published openly; the protective claims relate to specific protocol mechanisms whose disclosure would compromise the security model.

The standard covers:

- The canonical payload format that devices must produce
- The commitment scheme that binds payload bytes to a fixed-size cryptographic digest
- The signing semantics — what is signed, with what key material, by what process
- The public-input structure that on-chain verifiers receive
- The verification rules that must hold for a submission to be accepted

IPAS is the **interoperability layer**. Any device, any aggregator, any verifier can participate in the protocol so long as it follows the standard. InfraVeritas Energy is the first reference implementation; it is not intended to be the only one.

### 3.3 Hardware root of trust

The device layer is bound by hardware. Edge devices in the InfraVeritas protocol use commodity hardware security modules — initially the Microchip ATECC608B family — in which the device's private signing key is generated on-chip during manufacturing and never leaves the chip. The chip exposes a signing operation; it does not expose the key.

This places the device's cryptographic identity outside the reach of remote attackers, firmware-replacement attacks, and most classes of supply-chain interference. A compromised host operating system cannot extract the key; it can only request signatures, and those signatures are logged on-chain through the on-chain device registry, making misuse detectable.

The hardware root of trust is also the basis for **device authorization**. Each device's public key is registered on-chain at deployment time by an authorized operator. The registry contract enforces that only registered, currently-active devices can produce accepted submissions. Devices can be deactivated — without recovering the key — through an on-chain administrative action.

### 3.4 Cryptographic commitment

The measurement layer is bound by a deterministic commitment scheme. Each measurement payload — sensor readings, geolocation, timestamp, device state — is canonicalized into a fixed-format byte sequence and hashed into a 32-byte digest using a Poseidon-family hash function tuned for compatibility with zero-knowledge proof circuits.

The commitment is signed by the device's hardware-rooted key. From that point forward, any alteration of the measurement payload is cryptographically detectable: the commitment no longer matches the data, and the signature no longer validates against the commitment.

The choice of Poseidon over more common hash functions (SHA-256, Keccak) is not aesthetic. Poseidon is approximately three orders of magnitude cheaper to evaluate inside a zero-knowledge proof circuit than SHA-256 — a difference that directly determines whether per-measurement proofs are economically viable.

### 3.5 Zero-knowledge proof binding

The submission layer is bound by a zero-knowledge proof that connects the device, the measurement, and the on-chain public inputs.

For each submission, the aggregator generates a proof attesting that:

1. The device's signature validates against the committed measurement
2. The measurement payload, when canonicalized, produces the committed digest
3. The public values revealed to the on-chain verifier are consistent with the private measurement payload

The proof is verified on-chain by a Honk-family verifier contract. Successful verification means — to the smart contract consuming the data — that all three trust layers have been validated, end-to-end, without trusting the aggregator or any other off-chain actor. The aggregator orchestrates; it does not vouch.

The proof itself reveals only the public inputs declared by the protocol — device identifier, session identifier, timestamp, location, declared measurement aggregates. The raw measurement payload, individual sensor readings, and device-internal state remain private. This is what makes the protocol both cryptographically strong and operationally privacy-preserving.

### 3.6 The protocol surface

The full data path is:

```
[Hardware-rooted device]
    │
    │ Signs Poseidon commitment of canonical payload
    ▼
[Edge canonicalization + commitment]
    │
    │ Submits {payload, signature, public_key} to aggregator
    ▼
[Aggregator: validation + ZK proof generation + cross-validation]
    │
    │ Submits {public_inputs, signature, pubkey, ZK proof} on-chain
    ▼
[V3 contract: signature verify + proof verify + device registry check]
    │
    │ Emits ProofSubmitted event
    ▼
[Smart contracts consuming verified physical-world data]
```

Each arrow in the diagram is a cryptographic binding, not a trust assumption. The aggregator can fail, the network can be compromised, the operator can be malicious — and the on-chain consumer still receives data that is either cryptographically valid or rejected outright. There is no degraded-trust mode.

## 4. Technical Architecture

> *Working on Sepolia. End-to-end. Today.*

### 4.1 System overview

The InfraVeritas Energy implementation is structured as four cooperating components, each addressing a specific concern in the architecture described in Section 3:

| Component | Responsibility | Implementation |
|---|---|---|
| **Edge device** | Measurement, canonicalization, hardware-rooted signing | Python/Rust HAL targeting ESP32 + ATECC608B |
| **Aggregator** | Validation, witness generation, ZK proof production, on-chain submission | TypeScript/Node.js service, Postgres-backed |
| **V3 registry contract** | On-chain device registration, proof verification, event emission | Solidity 0.8.28, UUPS upgradeable proxy |
| **ZK verifier contract** | Honk proof verification | Generated from Noir circuit, deployed standalone |

The components communicate through well-defined interfaces — a wire protocol between edge and aggregator, an Ethereum RPC interface between aggregator and contracts, and a cryptographic protocol that connects all of them end-to-end.

### 4.2 V3 — The on-chain registry

The V3 contract is the canonical on-chain entry point. It is an upgradeable proxy (UUPS pattern) currently deployed on Sepolia at proxy address `0xF21D900E43214b0AbF489f8D6862352aaBB09DA3`. The proxy delegates to a versioned implementation contract — the current implementation is v0.3.1, supporting EIP-712 typed data infrastructure for future signed-message operations.

V3 enforces four verification checks on every submission:

1. **Device authorization** — the submitting device's public key must be registered and currently active in the on-chain DeviceRegistry
2. **Signature validity** — the device's P-256 signature must verify against the committed payload digest
3. **Proof validity** — the accompanying zero-knowledge proof must verify against the public inputs and the deployed circuit
4. **Temporal consistency** — the submission's epoch must satisfy the contract's strict-monotonic gap check, preventing replay

A successful submission emits a `ProofSubmitted` event that downstream contracts and indexers consume.

### 4.3 The ZK verification layer

Proof generation uses the Honk proof system, compiled from a Noir circuit that encodes the IPAS verification rules. The circuit validates:

- That the Poseidon hash of the canonical payload equals the committed payload hash
- That the public metadata fields match the corresponding payload positions
- That the device's signature is valid for the committed hash under the registered public key
- That declared measurement aggregates are consistent with the underlying readings

On-chain verification is performed by a generated Honk verifier contract. Per-proof gas cost is approximately 250,000–300,000 on Ethereum L1 — within the economic threshold at which per-measurement attestation is practical.

The choice of Honk over earlier proof systems (Groth16, Plonk) reflects two priorities: trust-setup minimality and verifier composability. Honk requires only a transparent universal setup, which removes the operational burden of maintaining a project-specific ceremony, and its verifier integrates cleanly with Solidity contracts in a way that earlier systems did not.

### 4.4 The aggregator

The aggregator is the orchestration layer. It accepts submissions from edge devices, performs validation (including a fast pre-check of the device's signature before committing computational resources), generates the ZK witness from the validated submission, produces the Honk proof, performs cross-validation against external data sources where applicable, and finally submits the proof to V3 on-chain.

The aggregator is **not a trust anchor**. Its outputs are cryptographically verified at every consuming layer. If the aggregator misbehaves — generates an invalid proof, alters payload data, fabricates submissions — the on-chain verifier rejects the submission. The aggregator's compromise is a denial-of-service condition, not an integrity violation.

Cross-validation is a defense-in-depth layer. For energy generation submissions, the aggregator can optionally consult independent weather data providers and flag submissions whose claimed generation is inconsistent with environmental conditions. This does not gate on-chain submission — the cryptographic proof determines admissibility — but it surfaces anomalies for operator review.

### 4.5 The edge HAL

Edge devices run a hardware abstraction layer (HAL) that exposes a uniform API across deployment targets. The HAL handles sensor reading, payload canonicalization, Poseidon commitment computation, and hardware-mediated signing through the on-board HSM.

The reference HAL is implemented in Python for development and testing, with a parallel Rust implementation targeting production deployment on ESP32-class microcontrollers. The two implementations share canonicalization and commitment logic byte-for-byte; the Python version uses software P-256 signing for development convenience, while the Rust version routes signing through the ATECC608B for production.

## 5. Use Cases

> *Energy is just the first asset class.*

InfraVeritas Energy is named for its initial vertical, but the underlying protocol is general-purpose. Any physical measurement that can be produced by an authorized device can flow through the same cryptographic pipeline. The architecture is asset-agnostic; only the canonicalization rules and circuit constraints are vertical-specific.

### 5.1 Renewable energy tokenization

The primary launch market is tokenization of distributed renewable energy generation — predominantly solar, with extension paths to small-scale wind and hydro. The verification gap in this market is acute: tokenized solar yields require continuous, trustworthy measurement of generation; current solutions either rely on certified installers (high cost) or unverified self-reporting (low trust). InfraVeritas Energy replaces both with cryptographically verified measurement at the device.

The economic threshold is favorable. A residential 10 kW solar installation produces 12,000–15,000 kWh annually. Even at modest tokenization premiums, the per-installation revenue covers per-measurement verification costs by two orders of magnitude.

### 5.2 DePIN sensor networks

Decentralized Physical Infrastructure Network (DePIN) projects depend on trustworthy data from distributed sensors — air quality, network performance, environmental telemetry. The dominant model today rewards sensor operators based on self-reported uptime and unverified measurements, which has produced well-documented gaming (synthetic data, fake devices, location spoofing).

InfraVeritas Energy's verification primitive translates directly into DePIN trust layers. A DePIN network that adopts IPAS-compliant attestation produces sensor data with the same cryptographic guarantees as energy generation submissions: device authorization, measurement integrity, submission integrity. Operator reward distribution can be made cryptographically conditional rather than reputation-based.

### 5.3 Permissioned RWA via ERC-3643

The protocol integrates with the ERC-3643 permissioned token standard, the leading framework for regulated real-world asset tokenization. ERC-3643's compliance modules enforce identity, jurisdiction, and accreditation constraints on token holders; InfraVeritas Energy adds the missing piece — cryptographic attestation that the underlying physical asset corresponds to what the token claims.

The combination produces a complete on-chain primitive for regulated RWA: holder eligibility verified by ERC-3643 compliance, asset reality verified by InfraVeritas Energy.

### 5.4 Forward verticals

Beyond the initial markets, several asset categories are natural extensions of the same protocol:

- **Carbon credit attestation** — replacing trusted verifier signatures with device-rooted measurement of biomass, soil carbon, or industrial offsets
- **Infrastructure performance bonds** — tokenized debt instruments whose payouts depend on verifiable physical performance (bridge load capacity, building energy efficiency)
- **Environmental compliance** — regulatory submissions backed by tamper-evident sensor records rather than self-reported claims
- **Insurance parametric triggers** — payouts tied to cryptographically verified physical conditions rather than third-party adjusters

Each extension reuses the same architectural primitives. Only the canonicalization rules and the specific measurements change.

## 6. Compliance & Standards

> *Built compliant from day one.*

Regulatory compatibility is not an afterthought. The protocol's architectural choices — particularly the on-chain device authorization model, the privacy-preserving public input structure, and the integration with ERC-3643 — were made with explicit reference to current and pending regulatory frameworks in the European Union and the United States.

### 6.1 MiCA alignment

The protocol is designed to satisfy the verification and recordkeeping obligations of MiCA Article 36 (asset-referenced tokens) and Article 45 (operational resilience). The current implementation includes:

- A formal mapping from IPAS verification events to MiCA Article 45 incident classification
- Privacy-preserving but auditable submission records, accessible to authorized regulators via standard Ethereum tooling
- Documented incident response procedures aligned with European Banking Authority operational guidance

The MiCA mapping is published as compliance documentation in the project repository. The mapping has not yet been independently reviewed by qualified counsel; that review is scheduled as part of Phase 6 production hardening.

### 6.2 GDPR Privacy-by-Design

The protocol is structured to avoid putting any personally identifiable information on-chain. Device public keys, session identifiers, timestamps, and aggregate measurements are public; raw measurement payloads, individual sensor readings, and any operator-identifying metadata remain off-chain.

This satisfies the GDPR Privacy-by-Design requirement (Article 25) and avoids the well-documented tension between GDPR's right-to-erasure provisions (Article 17) and the immutability of public blockchains. The aggregator layer retains operator-identifying records subject to standard data retention controls; the on-chain record contains only cryptographic commitments.

GDPR compliance documentation is published in the project repository alongside the MiCA mapping.

### 6.3 ERC-3643 integration

The protocol is designed for native composition with the ERC-3643 permissioned token standard. InfraVeritas Energy provides physical asset verification; ERC-3643 provides holder compliance. The two combine into a complete primitive for regulated real-world asset tokenization.

The integration surface is intentionally minimal — ERC-3643 modules can consume `ProofSubmitted` events from V3 as triggers for token-level operations (minting against verified generation, redemption against verified asset state, etc.) without requiring modification to either contract.

### 6.4 Intellectual property posture

The protocol's source code is published under a Source-Available license that permits inspection, evaluation, and academic use while reserving commercial deployment rights pending negotiated licensing. The IPAS standard itself is under USPTO patent protection (Provisional Application #63/876,031), with the utility patent filing window open through August 2026.

The combination — open standard, source-available reference implementation, protected core mechanisms — is intentional. The protocol is designed to be independently implementable by other teams (multiple compatible implementations strengthen rather than threaten the standard), while the protective claims ensure that the foundational mechanisms remain available for long-term ecosystem stewardship rather than being absorbed into a single commercial silo.

## 7. Roadmap

> *From working proof to production-ready in 12 months.*

The protocol is currently at the close of Phase 5 of an eight-phase architectural roadmap. Phases 0 through 5 represent the path from initial design to working testnet with comprehensive cross-validation, monitoring, and compliance documentation. Phases 6 through 8 represent the path from working testnet to production mainnet deployment.

### Completed (Phases 0–5)

- **Phase 0 — Foundations.** Core architectural design, IPAS standard v1.0 specification, patent provisional filing.
- **Phase 1 — Sepolia end-to-end pipeline.** First fully working pipeline from edge device through aggregator to on-chain verification (closed May 2026).
- **Phase 2 — Hardware root of trust.** ESP32 + ATECC608B firmware (in active development on a parallel hardware track).
- **Phase 3 — Operational baseline.** Excluded from MVP scope; multisig and timelock controls reserved for Phase 6.
- **Phase 4 — Cross-validation layer.** Weather API ensemble validation, anomaly detection, persistence (closed May 2026).
- **Phase 5 — Monitoring and compliance.** Subgraph indexing, dual-mode operational dashboard, compliance documentation package (MiCA mapping, GDPR review, incident response playbook) (closed May 2026).

### Next 12 months (Phases 6–8)

**Phase 6 — Production hardening.** Migration of administrative controls from single-signer to multisig (Gnosis Safe), introduction of timelock on upgrade operations, hardware wallet integration for operator keys, monitoring infrastructure for production-grade alerting, runbook documentation for incident response. Estimated duration: 3–4 months.

**Phase 7 — External audit.** Engagement with a recognized smart contract auditing firm, remediation of audit findings, qualified counsel review of MiCA and GDPR compliance documentation. Estimated duration: 2–3 months including remediation.

**Phase 8 — Mainnet deployment.** Production deployment of V3 and supporting contracts on Ethereum mainnet, operational handoff procedures, initial production device deployment with launch partner operators, documentation publication. Estimated duration: 1–2 months.

### Beyond mainnet

The 12-month roadmap establishes production capability. The longer-term direction extends in two complementary directions:

- **Vertical expansion** — IPAS-compliant implementations for asset categories beyond energy, beginning with carbon credit attestation and DePIN sensor networks
- **Standard adoption** — engagement with the ERC-3643 Association, OpenWallet Foundation, and energy industry bodies to position IPAS as a recognized industry standard

The IPAS standard's USPTO utility patent filing must be completed by August 2026; this timing aligns naturally with the close of Phase 6.

The grant ecosystem (Energy Web Foundation, Ethereum Ecosystem Support Program, Octant, and parallel initiatives at the major Ethereum L2s) has explicitly funded prior protocols at this stage of maturity. InfraVeritas Energy is, by any reasonable measure, further along the maturity curve at the moment of ask than most projects historically funded by these programs.
